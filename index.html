QA Automation with LLMs

Cox Enterprises
2026
LLM automation Claude · Copilot Tosca · Python

Test authoring was the release bottleneck. Every feature meant hand writing cases from requirements before building them into Tosca, and coverage gaps surfaced late in regression where they cost the most. I built a workflow around LLMs in front of the automation framework with two parts: test case generation from requirements, and retrieval over the Tosca module library and past defect history to surface coverage gaps before a release decision. A person stayed in the loop where judgment mattered.

  • What I fed it

    I started by feeding raw requirement documents and full user stories, but drafts came back generic and low value. I switched to feeding structured acceptance criteria plus field level constraints only, which produced far sharper test cases.

  • What I rejected

    The model never wrote directly into the Tosca suite. Generated cases sat behind a human review gate, because roughly one in three came back as duplicates of existing coverage or invalid once real system state was accounted for.

  • The judgment

    The value was not AI writing the tests. It was clearing the blank page step so I could focus on which edge cases carried real risk and which cases would block a release. Copilot handled inline drafting in the IDE and Claude handled longer requirement documents.

  • What changed

    The final flow ran structured criteria into draft cases into a review gate into Tosca modules. Authoring time dropped by roughly a third, coverage gaps surfaced before regression instead of during it, and defect metrics fed straight into release decisions.

  • Value

    Freed QA capacity from the slowest part of the release cycle and moved coverage gaps earlier, where defects are cheaper to fix, giving the Delivery Team faster and better informed release decisions.

Built a production ready pipeline that turns unstructured free text into reliable predictions, then layered an agentic LLM framework on top to keep the data feeding it trustworthy.

  • What I fed it

    Unstructured free text, vectorized with TF-IDF into features an XGBoost classifier could learn from. The classes were imbalanced, so I applied SMOTE to synthesize minority examples and stop the model from being swamped by the majority class.

  • What I rejected

    I did not trust a single train and test score. I rejected models that looked good on paper but failed under cross validation or on the precision, recall, and ROC/PR curves, using those and feature importance to keep only what held up.

  • The judgment

    High precision mattered more than raw accuracy here, so I tuned toward it and built the workflow to be modular and production ready rather than a one off notebook, so it could be rerun and trusted.

  • What changed

    The result was a transparent, reliable classification workflow. On top of it I led an agentic LLM framework for data integrity checking, creating synthetic supervision data and structured inference outputs to support auditability, fine tuning, and scalable deployment.

  • Value

    Turned messy free text into a signal the business could act on, with a workflow transparent enough to trust and an integrity layer that kept the inputs clean at scale.

Predictive Irrigation Water Budget on OpenET

Agriculture · Water Conservation
In progress
OpenET APIForecastingConvex Optimization

Irrigation accounts for the large majority of consumptive water use in the American West, and much of it is still scheduled by calendar rather than by what the crop actually needs. This schedules water against forecasted demand, then allocates a capped drought budget across fields to maximize total yield per drop.

  • Why

    Watering on a fixed calendar overwaters in cool weeks and underwaters in hot ones. Reactive scheduling that responds only once crop stress appears is no better, since by then both water and yield are already lost. The useful position is in between: forecast demand a week out and irrigate to it, and under a cap treat water as a budget to allocate rather than a per field decision made in isolation.

  • Data & APIs

    Field geometry posts to the OpenET API, a NASA backed service that returns satellite derived evapotranspiration at quarter acre resolution from an ensemble of models. I pull actual ET, reference ET, and precipitation per field, then derive the crop coefficient (ETa over ETo) as the state variable. NOAA forecasts supply forward temperature, humidity, wind, and solar radiation.

  • Modeling

    A gradient boosted regressor forecasts next period reference ET from the weather features, and the crop coefficient carries the field's own phenology. Predicted demand is ETc minus effective rainfall, run as a rolling soil water balance so the model tracks depletion against available water rather than resetting each period. Backtesting is walk forward on prior seasons to avoid leaking future weather into past predictions.

  • Allocation

    Yield response to water is concave, with sharp gains while a crop is stressed and diminishing returns near full demand. That makes the allocation a convex problem, so a water filling routine assigns each marginal unit to whichever field gains the most area weighted yield from it, until the budget is exhausted. The output is a per field depth in millimeters, a fill percentage against demand, and gallons saved versus a calendar baseline.

  • Value

    Because the allocator concentrates cuts where the yield penalty is smallest, a constrained budget loses far less production than an even percentage cut across every field. Trimming consumptive use even a few percent across a district leaves real water in stressed basins and lowers pumping energy and cost at the same time.

Carbon Aware Task Scheduler Agent

Energy · Grid Decarbonization
Prototype
Carbon Intensity APILLM Tool UsePython · Gradio

Grid carbon intensity swings widely across a single day, so the same kilowatt hour can carry several times the emissions depending on when it is drawn. This takes a flexible task described in plain language and schedules it into the cleanest window that still meets its deadline.

  • Why

    Running immediately is simplest but lands on whatever the grid happens to be burning. Waiting for the single greenest hour is cleanest but misses deadlines, which is why naive green scheduling gets switched off in practice. Treating the deadline as a hard constraint and carbon as the objective keeps the savings without breaking the workflow.

  • Data & APIs

    A 24 hour forward forecast in 30 minute slots comes from the National Grid Carbon Intensity API, which is public and needs no key. Because a live dependency should never take a demo down, the client degrades to a synthetic diurnal curve modeling the evening peak, the midday solar dip, and the overnight trough, so the optimizer path stays testable offline.

  • Optimizer

    Scheduling reduces to a minimum mean sliding window over the forecast: for a task spanning n slots, evaluate every contiguous window whose end index falls before the deadline and take the lowest average intensity. Linear in the forecast length, deterministic, and covered by a self test asserting the chosen window never scores worse than running immediately.

  • Agent layer

    An LLM parses the plain language request and calls the scheduler through a typed tool schema, extracting duration and deadline into structured arguments. The model chooses inputs and delegates execution; the optimizer stays the single source of truth, so the schedule is never a hallucinated time. A deterministic parser backs it up when no API key is present.

  • Value

    On representative daily curves, shifting a multi hour job inside a same day deadline cuts its emissions by roughly a quarter to a half, with no hardware and no change to the work itself. The same pattern scales from a laptop training run to data center batch jobs and smart appliance load.
